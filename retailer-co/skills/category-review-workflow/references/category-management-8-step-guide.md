# Category Management 8-Step Process Guide

Industry-standard framework for managing retail categories. Based on the Category Management Association (CMA) process adapted for CPG natural/specialty retail.

## Overview

The 8-step process is the standard methodology used by retailers worldwide to manage product categories as strategic business units. Each step builds on the previous one, creating a structured approach from definition through measurement.

## Step 1: Category Definition

**Purpose**: Define the category boundaries and internal structure.

**Key activities**:
- Identify all products that belong in the category from the consumer's perspective
- Map the consumer decision tree (how shoppers navigate the category)
- Define sub-categories and segments
- Identify adjacencies (related categories that influence this one)

**Consumer decision tree example — Plant-Based Milk**:
```
Plant-Based Milk
├── By Base (primary decision)
│   ├── Oat
│   ├── Almond
│   ├── Soy
│   ├── Coconut
│   └── Other (cashew, pea, hemp)
├── By Use (secondary decision)
│   ├── Drinking / cereal
│   ├── Coffee creamer
│   └── Cooking / baking
├── By Attribute (tertiary decision)
│   ├── Original / unsweetened
│   ├── Vanilla / flavored
│   └── Barista / specialty
└── By Brand (final decision)
    ├── National brands
    └── Store brands
```

**Output**: Category definition document with product list, sub-segments, and decision tree.

## Step 2: Category Role

**Purpose**: Determine the category's strategic importance to the store.

**Four standard roles**:

### Destination
- The store is known for this category
- Consumers choose the store specifically for this category
- Deserves maximum assortment breadth and depth
- Pricing must be competitive or best-in-market
- Example: Organic produce at Whole Foods

### Routine
- Regular, expected purchase — consumers expect it to be available
- Standard assortment meets most needs
- Pricing competitive with market
- Example: Conventional dairy at a conventional grocer

### Seasonal / Occasional
- Driven by time of year, holidays, or events
- Assortment flexes by season
- Promotional focus during peak periods
- Example: Grilling items in summer

### Convenience
- Fill-in purchase, not a trip driver
- Limited assortment of top sellers
- Standard margins, minimal promotional investment
- Example: Office supplies at a grocery store

**Role assignment criteria**:
- Consumer research (why do shoppers come to your store?)
- Competitive positioning (where do you want to differentiate?)
- Financial contribution (what role does this category play in store P&L?)
- Market trends (is this category growing or declining?)

## Step 3: Category Assessment

**Purpose**: Establish the current performance baseline.

**Data required**:
- Category sales, units, and margin (your stores)
- Category sales and growth (total market — SPINS/IRI/Nielsen)
- Market share by brand and segment
- Price comparison vs key competitors
- Distribution coverage (% of stores carrying each item)
- Promotional history and effectiveness
- Vendor performance scorecards

**Key questions**:
- Are we growing faster or slower than the market?
- Which brands and segments are driving growth?
- Where are our assortment gaps vs the market?
- Which SKUs are underperforming?
- How effective are our promotions vs industry benchmarks?

## Step 4: Category Scorecard

**Purpose**: Set measurable targets for the category.

**Standard KPIs**:

| KPI | What It Measures | Typical Target Setting |
|-----|-----------------|----------------------|
| Sales growth % | Revenue trend | At or above market growth rate |
| Unit velocity/store/week | Product movement | Category-specific benchmarks |
| Gross margin % | Profitability | Role-dependent (higher for convenience, lower for destination) |
| SKU productivity | Revenue per SKU | Minimum threshold to justify shelf space |
| Out-of-stock rate | Availability | Below 2% for destination, below 5% for routine |
| New item success rate | Innovation effectiveness | 60%+ of new items exceeding velocity threshold at 90 days |
| Promotional ROI | Promo effectiveness | Incremental lift exceeds trade spend |

## Step 5: Category Strategy

**Purpose**: Set the strategic direction based on role and assessment.

**Strategy-to-role mapping**:

| Role | Primary Strategy | Secondary Strategy |
|------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Destination | Traffic builder | Image enhancer |
| Routine | Transaction builder | Cash generator |
| Seasonal | Traffic builder (in season) | Cash generator (off season) |
| Convenience | Cash generator | — |

## Step 6: Category Tactics

**Purpose**: Translate strategy into specific actions across the four tactical levers.

### Assortment tactics
- Add items that fill documented gaps
- Remove items below minimum velocity/productivity thresholds
- Adjust segment mix to align with consumer demand
- Introduce store brand items where margin opportunity exists

### Pricing tactics
- Set everyday prices relative to competitive set and category role
- Identify value perception drivers (the items shoppers use to judge your prices)
- Set price gaps between national brands and store brands

### Promotion tactics
- Align promotional frequency with category role
- Set promotional depth (% off) based on lift data
- Coordinate promotional calendar with vendor funding commitments
- Test promotional types (TPR vs endcap vs circular vs digital)

### Shelf tactics
- Allocate facings based on velocity and margin contribution
- Set shelf flow to match consumer decision tree
- Position high-margin items at eye level
- Ensure new items have trial placement

## Step 7: Plan Implementation

**Purpose**: Execute all tactical changes.

**Implementation timeline** (typical):
- Week 1-2: Finalize tactical plan, get approvals
- Week 3-4: Communicate changes to vendors, submit pricing changes
- Week 5-6: Prepare planogram resets, schedule store execution
- Week 7-8: Execute resets, go live with new assortment and pricing
- Week 9+: Monitor and adjust

## Step 8: Category Review

**Purpose**: Measure results and iterate.

**Review cadence**:
- 30-day check: Are changes executing as planned? Any fires to put out?
- 60-day review: Early performance indicators. Adjust tactics if needed.
- 90-day review: Full performance assessment against scorecard. Document learnings.
- Annual review: Full cycle assessment. Input to next category plan.

**Key principle**: Category management is continuous, not episodic. The 8-step process is a cycle, not a one-time event. Each review feeds the next round of assessment and strategy.
